Knowledge Management with Obsidian

By Dr Daft | June 15, 2023 | 4 minutes
life writing research-life

Retaining information is difficult at the best of times let alone considering what happens to us with age. I have an obscure fear of loosing my mind! As silly as that might sound there is a word for it dementophobia or the fear of madness. Perhaps because of seeing my grandmother slowly rewrite their own past before my eyes or some other as of yet unearthed psychological reason, I do find myself thinking about this very phobia and what would become. I’ve dedicated a portion of my life to knowledge and education so yes I would like to retain that knowledge, but what happens when it’s too much?

I love referring back to John Koenigs’s Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows for possible ways to define the human condition because the English language is too clinical. I come from a culture of metaphors, Urdu the language I speak is filled with it and feels poetic for this reason. While there are too many words in Koenig’s work that might fit my human condition in the myriad obscurities that make up who I am, I feel what works best with me in the context of this writing is koinophobia.

Koinophobia, n. the fear that you’ve lived an ordinary life.

â€” The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows, John Koenig

This might be an over-self-analysis but I think the reason why I want to retain information is because I want to not be ordinary, or at the very least give life purpose. My wife often says I have the most strangest of facts in my head and that is quite true, what use is knowing that Australia is wider than the moon?1

This is all of course a meaningless fear I carry, I can’t possibly retain all information. I am human and I am supposed to err, but I can still do the best with what I have or try to at least. Here I’d like to walk through what my process of retaining information and utilising it effectively has become using Obsidian, an ever evolving second-brain as many productivity enthusiasts like to refer to as.

The second-brain I always longed for

In my last post on knowledge management I went on about how my profession demanded that I write and expand on knowledge. Clearly that isn’t the only reason why I’ve opted for using something like Obsidian. It is deeply personal as well and rooted in this interest of improving myself. I kept paper notes for a long time and still do, the feel of lead and paper are something no Apple pencil can ever recreate, Obsidian acts in a different way it enters this fold as a means to make sense of the scribbles I’ve put down in my notebook.

The second-brain concept is essentially a mindset, you don’t need one if your work and habits don’t require this approach. But for those who have struggled with retaining information or are sitting on a pile of collected notes in various notebooks, this might just be what you need because it was what I needed. Obsidan is a note taking tool like Notion and others that are in the market. When people swore by these tools I found them ridiculous but having now used Obsidian for a while I fully understand the benefits. I should give a forewarning though, this is a slippery rabbit hole and you can get lost into! I would suggest to take from here what makes sense to you, no two approaches of knowledge management are the same or will work universally and you will need to make them your own.

Obsidian uses Markdown (MD) a no frills approach towards writing that lets you focus on content rather than appearance. I’m not going to explain how to write in MD though here is a handy interactive tutorial that should teach you the basics super fast.

Sidebar: I fully support the use of MD and think it is the best way for any academic to be writing. While Latex has its pros in creating a final output, it is cumbersome in the process and I think should be left for the very end of the publishing process. On that note if you are someone familiar with Latex then rest assured you can convert from MD quite effectively.
